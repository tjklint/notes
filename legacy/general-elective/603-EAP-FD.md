### Exercise 1.1.2: Key Education Terms

- **Critical Thinking:**  
  A disciplined process of analyzing and evaluating information.
- **Higher Order Critical Thinking Skills:**  
  Involves analysis, synthesis, and evaluation rather than simple recall.
- **Disciplinary Learning vs. Interdisciplinary Learning:**  
  - *Disciplinary:* Focus on one subject at a time.  
  - *Interdisciplinary:* Integrates multiple subjects (e.g., biology, English) to provide a holistic perspective.
- **Critical Self-Reflection:**  
  Examining one’s biases and beliefs through deep analysis.
- **Curriculum & Learning Styles:**  
  - *Curriculum:* Organized learning program.  
  - *Project-based/Active Learning:* Student-centered, inquiry-driven approach that addresses real-world issues.

### Critical Thinking and Interdisciplinarity

- Critical thinking is essential for tackling complex environmental challenges.
- Interdisciplinary approaches nurture higher-order thinking, encouraging students to integrate knowledge, reflect on personal biases, and develop independent solutions.

### Exercise 1.1.3: Language Registers

- **Technical-Formal:**
  - Uses precise, technical language (e.g., “ethnographic study”, “social constructivist pedagogy”).
  - Avoids contractions.
- **Formal:**
  - Grammatically correct and structured; employs both technical terms and accessible language.
- **Casual-Formal:**
  - A blend of relaxed tone (including contractions and personal voice) with precise language, making content both engaging and clear.
- **Comparative Insights:**
  - Different texts (Wason-Ellam, McMillan & Vasseur, Smith & Sobel) illustrate how language can shift from highly technical to more personal or relaxed styles while still conveying academic content.
